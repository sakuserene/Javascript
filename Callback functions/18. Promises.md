What Is a Promise?
Think about the word promise in everyday life.
Imagine your friend says:
"I promise I'll bring your laptop tomorrow."
At that moment:
You don't have the laptop yet.
You don't know whether your friend will keep the promise.
You only know that a result will come later.
There are two possible outcomes:
✅ Your friend brings the laptop.
❌ Your friend doesn't bring the laptop.
This is exactly how a JavaScript Promise works.
A Promise represents a value that is not available yet but will be available in the future.
Real-World Example 1 — Online Food Order
You order food online.
Immediately after placing the order:
You Place Order
      ↓
Restaurant Receives Order
      ↓
Food Is Being Prepared
At this moment, you don't have the food.
The order is still being prepared.
This is called the Pending state.
Eventually, one of two things happens.
Option 1 — Success
Order Placed
      ↓
Food Prepared
      ↓
Driver Delivers Food
      ↓
You Receive Food
The Promise is Fulfilled.
Option 2 — Failure
Order Placed
      ↓
Restaurant Runs Out of Food
      ↓
Order Cancelled
The Promise is Rejected.
The Three States of a Promise
Every Promise can only be in one of three states.
1. Pending
The operation has started.
It has not finished yet.
Example:
Downloading a file...
2. Fulfilled (Resolved)
The operation finished successfully.
Example:
Download Complete
3. Rejected
The operation failed.
Example:
Internet Connection Lost
Promise Lifecycle
Every Promise follows the same journey.
Create Promise
      ↓
Pending
      ↓
 ┌───────────────┐
 │               │
 ▼               ▼
Fulfilled     Rejected
Notice something important.
A Promise can never go backwards.
For example:
Pending
      ↓
Fulfilled
It can never become Pending again.
Likewise:
Pending
      ↓
Rejected
It stays rejected.
A Promise changes state only once.
Real-World Example 2 — Job Application
You apply for a job.
Immediately after submitting:
Application Submitted
      ↓
Waiting
This is Pending.
Later:
Option 1
Congratulations!
You got the job.
Fulfilled.
Option 2
Sorry.
You were not selected.
Rejected.
Why Were Promises Introduced?
Remember Callback Hell?
createUser(function () {

    verifyEmail(function () {

        login(function () {

            loadDashboard(function () {

                showWelcome();

            });

        });

    });

});
This becomes difficult to read.
Promises allow us to write:
createUser()
    .then(verifyEmail)
    .then(login)
    .then(loadDashboard)
    .then(showWelcome)
    .catch(handleError);
Notice how the code now flows from top to bottom instead of moving further to the right.
Promise vs Callback
Callback approach:
Task 1
    ↓
    Task 2
        ↓
        Task 3
            ↓
            Task 4
Promise approach:
Task 1
      ↓
Task 2
      ↓
Task 3
      ↓
Task 4
Much easier to read.
Important Point
A Promise does not execute work by itself.
It only represents the result of work.
Think of it like this.
A restaurant receipt is not the food.
It is proof that food is being prepared.
Similarly:
A Promise is not the final value.
It represents a value that will become available later.
Another Analogy — Parcel Delivery
You order a phone online.
Immediately:
Order Confirmed
You don't have the phone.
The package is moving through different stages.
Order Confirmed
      ↓
Packed
      ↓
Shipped
      ↓
Out for Delivery
      ↓
Delivered
Until delivery, you're waiting.
That's the Pending state.
When delivered:
Fulfilled.
If the package is lost:
Rejected.

Summary
A Promise is:
An object.
A placeholder for a future value.
Used for asynchronous operations.
Every Promise has exactly three possible states:
Pending
      ↓
Fulfilled
or
Pending
      ↓
Rejected
It can never return to Pending.
